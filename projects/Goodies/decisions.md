## Decisions I made

### I chose curation over breadth

From the beginning, I decided Goodies would not try to carry everything.

Assortment was constrained intentionally.

This meant:
- Saying no to products that were interesting but incoherent
- Limiting category expansion until systems could support it
- Curating depth within categories rather than maximizing SKU count

The goal was not completeness.
It was trust.

Customers should be able to assume that if something is on the shelf, it passed a clear standard.

---

### I chose margin discipline over volume signaling

I treated margin targets as a design constraint, not a financial afterthought.

This decision shaped:
- What products qualified
- How pricing was set
- When promotions were avoided
- Which products were allowed to take up physical space

I accepted slower growth in exchange for sustainability.
Revenue without margin was not considered success.

---

### I chose operational clarity over constant novelty

Novelty is easy to add and expensive to maintain.

I decided that new products would only be introduced when:
- Replenishment rhythm could be sustained
- Staff could explain the product clearly
- Inventory risk was understood
- The product fit existing customer behavior

This reduced cognitive load for staff and customers.
It also protected cash flow.

---

### I chose repeatable routines over heroics

Goodies is designed to run on systems, not individual effort spikes.

I prioritized:
- Clear opening and closing routines
- Predictable stocking and restocking patterns
- Simple merchandising rules
- Stable staffing expectations

This allowed the store to remain consistent even when energy, time, or staffing fluctuated.

---

### I chose in-person experience over aggressive optimization

Some optimizations were intentionally left on the table.

I did not:
- Push staff to maximize throughput at the expense of tone
- Optimize the space purely for transaction speed
- Treat customers as units of conversion

The store was designed to feel human first.
Efficiency was pursued only where it did not damage trust.

---

## Tradeoffs I accepted

### Focus over maximal revenue

By limiting assortment and pacing growth, Goodies left money on the table.

The tradeoff:
- Fewer impulse-driven sales
- Slower expansion of product categories

The benefit:
- Higher customer trust
- Cleaner operations
- Less volatility in inventory decisions

---

### Discipline over flexibility

Operational standards reduced improvisation.

The tradeoff:
- Less spontaneous experimentation
- More constraint on staff autonomy in the moment

The benefit:
- Predictable experience
- Reduced errors
- Easier training and handoff

---

### Personal responsibility over early delegation

For a long time, I carried the operational load directly.

This was intentional.

The system needed to be understood end-to-end before being handed off.
Delegation without clarity would have transferred confusion, not ownership.

The tradeoff was personal time and fatigue.
The benefit was a coherent, teachable system.

---

## What I intentionally did not do

### I did not chase scale for its own sake

I did not:
- Expand hours without a sustainable labor model
- Add locations before understanding unit economics
- Treat growth as an automatic good

Scale was treated as a design question, not a goal.

---

### I did not separate brand from operations

I rejected the idea that brand lives in marketing and operations live elsewhere.

This meant:
- Brand decisions were constrained by operational reality
- Operational decisions were evaluated for brand impact

If a choice broke trust or clarity, it was rejected regardless of short-term upside.

---

### I did not optimize for metrics without context

Metrics were used as indicators, not mandates.

I did not:
- Chase vanity growth numbers
- Optimize for dashboards instead of experience
- Let short-term data override long-term coherence

Judgment remained primary.

---

## Why this matters

These decisions show how I operate under sustained pressure.

They demonstrate:
- Discipline in the face of tempting shortcuts
- Systems thinking applied to daily execution
- Comfort making tradeoffs without external validation
- Accountability for outcomes that cannot be abstracted away

Goodies works only if these decisions hold up every day.

That is the test.
